pre-race classification problems split into two very different types: highly predictable “success” outcomes and much less predictable “failure” outcomes.

The GLM ROC-AUC summary shows strong performance for points finish (0.8712) and especially podium finish (0.9255), while DNF probability is much weaker (0.5735). The exploratory podium-plus-slow-pit-stop GLM sits at 0.8692.


Points and podium finishes are structurally predictable using pre-race information, while DNFs are not. 
That makes intuitive sense: top-end race outcomes are strongly constrained by qualifying position, grid placement, and recent form, while DNFs are more heavily exposed to 
mechanical issues, race incidents, and other sources of variance that are harder to capture before the race begins.

For points finish specifically, the logistic regression (0.8712) slightly outperforms XGBoost (0.8686), which is a strong signal that the main predictive structure is already well captured by relatively simple relationships. 
That directly supports your broader thesis that model complexity should follow the data, not the other way around.

For podium finish, the two models are nearly tied, with XGBoost (0.9280) only barely above logistic regression (0.9255). 
Not a meaningful win for the tree-based model. Maybe further evidence that the data is highly structured and that the main gains are coming from feature quality, not from nonlinear complexity.

For DNF, logistic regression (0.5735) slightly beats XGBoost (0.5644), but both are weak. That’s actually a valuable result, not a failure. It suggests that DNFs are materially less predictable 
from pre-race observables, which is exactly the kind of domain insight a good sports analytics project should surface.

How to interpret the coefficients carefully

points-finish coefficient table shows negative effects for qualifying_position, driver_avg_finish_last5, and driver_avg_grid_last5, which is directionally sensible because lower numeric values are better in those variables.
The odds ratio for qualifying_position is about 0.889, implying that worse qualifying position reduces the odds of a points finish.

podium-finish coefficient table tells an even cleaner story: qualifying_position has the strongest negative effect among the main race-performance variables, with an odds ratio around 0.792, 
followed by driver_avg_grid_last5, constructor_avg_finish_last5, driver_avg_finish_last5, and grid_clean. That is exactly the type of “starting position dominates” evidence you want to emphasize.

For DNF, the biggest positive odds ratios are constructor_dnf_rate_last5 (2.8931) and driver_dnf_rate_last5 (2.8204). That’s a very nice result: reliability history matters even if the overall DNF prediction problem remains hard.

One note: the positive signs on the DNF-rate features in the points/podium models look counterintuitive at first glance, since higher DNF rates should generally hurt performance. That likely means one of two things: 
either those features are interacting with other strong performance proxies in a way that distorts isolated GLM coefficients, or the scaling / correlation structure is producing unstable signs. This is another reason to prefer the SHAP interpretation from the XGBoost models for directional storytelling, 
while using GLM coefficients mainly to show interpretability mechanics.

How to interpret the visual outputs:
The points-finish SHAP charts show qualifying_position, grid_clean, constructor_points_last5, driver_avg_grid_last5, and driver_points_last5 as the dominant features. That lines up very well with the race-outcome thesis.
The podium-finish SHAP charts are even more concentrated around qualifying and grid. qualifying_position and grid_clean are clearly the top drivers, with a sharper spread than in the points model. This supports the idea that the closer you get to the front of the field, the more important initial position becomes.
The DNF SHAP charts look much flatter and less intuitively structured, with year, grid_clean, driver_points_last5, constructor_points_last5, and a few environmental features appearing near the top. That matches the weaker model performance and is worth explicitly saying: the DNF model appears to pick up some signal, but not nearly enough to make DNF risk as predictable as points or podium outcomes.
The pit-stop group plots are also useful. In the podium PDF, page 5 shows lower podium rates under slow pit stops, and in the DNF PDF, page 5 shows a higher DNF rate under slow pit stops. this is association, not causation, and slow pit stops may themselves reflect already-chaotic race conditions.


points finish:
A simple logistic model and a tree-based classifier both performed strongly, with logistic regression slightly outperforming XGBoost. This suggests that points-finish probability is largely governed by structured, pre-race information rather than highly nonlinear interactions.

podium finish:
Podium finish was even more predictable than points finish, with both models achieving ROC-AUC above 0.92. Qualifying and grid position dominated both coefficient-based and SHAP-based interpretation, reinforcing the idea that elite outcomes in Formula 1 are heavily shaped before the race begins.

DNF probability:
DNF probability was substantially harder to predict. Reliability-related history variables mattered, but overall discrimination remained modest, indicating that race attrition is influenced by a larger share of unobserved or inherently volatile race-day events.

slow pit stop impact:
Slow pit stops were associated with worse race outcomes, including lower podium rates and higher DNF rates in exploratory summaries. These findings do not establish causality, but they provide a strong starting point for deeper strategy-focused modeling.
